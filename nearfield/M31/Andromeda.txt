The Andromeda Galaxy (M31), which lies at a distance of $\approximately
765$~kpc, is the closest large spiral galaxy neighbor of the Milky
Way. Along with its satellite galaxies, the Andromeda system
represents the only other laboratory within which we can study the
detailed distribution of dark matter using individual stars. The
$\approx 300$~kpc diameter halo of Andromeda is spread over $\approx$20
degrees across the northern sky. Ground-based imaging observations
have shown that the stellar distribution in the halo shows a complex
composite structure of streams and shells, evidence of a complex
accretion history over the last few Gyr. Spectroscopy will enable
the measurement of radial velocities and metallicities of individual
stars in the Andromeda halo, and can thus result in the identification
and separation of the many streams, determinations of the mass
within each stream (and its progrenitor), and thus the reconstruction
of the enclosed mass function of the dwarf galaxies stripped to
create this structure. In addition, Spec-S5 spectroscopy may also be able
to identify narrow streams associated with stripped globular clusters
in the inner halo of Andromeda [NEED TO CHECK THIS USING THE SYMPHONY MODELS].

Based on existing imaging, there are $>$3 million candidate halo
stars in Andromeda at magnitudes $z\le 23$. Spec-S5 observations
with a total exposure time of 6~hours (i.e., $12\times30$~min
exposures) will result in $\sigma(V_{\rm rad}\approx 6.5~{\rm
km\,s^{-1}}$. With 13,000 fibers covering a 2.2~deg field of view,
the northern component of Spec-S5 can observe {\underline{\it all}}
of these $z\le23$~mag stars in $\approx 150$ nights, resulting in
an unprecedented map of the accretion history and dark matter mass
distribution within Andromeda 
(e.g., Figure~\ref{fig:specs5_selection,fig:m31_satellite_detection}). 
We note that this will be only other galaxy (i.e., other than the Milky Way) 
within which we have ever been able to understand the dark matter 
distribution to this level of detail.
